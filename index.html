About Me

I love cellular and systems neuroscience, and I've got a thing for rock climbing.

What I'm Interested In

My research interests are rooted in the homeostatic self-organization of intact neural networks that support sensation, perception and cognition, and how appropriate information transmission in these systems is established during development and disrupted in disease.

Currently, I’m investigating the role of sleep and wake in chaperoning the interactions between distinct plasticity mechanisms. Left to my own devices, I want to ask questions about how synaptic homeostasis can shape emergent properties of neuronal networks, topologies that ultimately represent the building blocks of cognition.

Sleep Suppresses Brain Rebalancing

A prominent and competing theory is that sleep is important for re-balancing activity in brain networks that have been perturbed during learning while awake. Such “rebalancing” of brain activity involves homeostatic plasticity mechanisms that were first discovered at Brandeis University... Now, a study from the Turrigiano lab just published in the journal Cell shows that these homeostatic mechanisms are indeed gated by sleep and wake, but in the opposite direction from that theorized previously

October 2013

Neuron

The Downs and Ups of Sensory Deprivation: Evidence for Firing Rate Homeostasis In Vivo

Homeostatic adjustment of neuronal firing rates is considered a vital mechanism to keep neurons operating in their optimal range despite dynamically changing input. Two studies in this issue of Neuron, Hengen et al. (2013) and Keck et al. (2013), provide evidence for firing rate homeostasis in the neocortex of freely behaving rodents.
