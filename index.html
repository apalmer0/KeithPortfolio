What I'm Interested In

My research interests are rooted in the homeostatic self-organization of intact neural networks that support sensation, perception and cognition, and how appropriate information transmission in these systems is established during development and disrupted in disease.

Currently, I’m investigating the role of sleep and wake in chaperoning the interactions between distinct plasticity mechanisms. Left to my own devices, I want to ask questions about how synaptic homeostasis can shape emergent properties of neuronal networks, topologies that ultimately represent the building blocks of cognition.

Sleep Suppresses Brain Rebalancing

A prominent and competing theory is that sleep is important for re-balancing activity in brain networks that have been perturbed during learning while awake. Such “rebalancing” of brain activity involves homeostatic plasticity mechanisms that were first discovered at Brandeis University... Neuron

The Downs and Ups of Sensory Deprivation: Evidence for Firing Rate Homeostasis In Vivo

Homeostatic adjustment of neuronal firing rates is considered a vital mechanism to keep neurons operating in their optimal range despite dynamically changing input. Two studies in this issue of Neuron, Hengen et al. (2013) and Keck et al. (2013), provide evidence for firing rate homeostasis in the neocortex of freely behaving rodents.

Skills & Expertise

To keep things simple, I have a history of asking questions that require an intersection of in vivo electrophysiology, genetics, cell biology, and computational modeling.

Neuroscience

From brainstem respiratory circuitry in hibernating mammals to general principals of plasticity in cortical microcircuits, I've been fascinated and humbled by all brain-related questions for more than a decade. New tools in systems neuroscience allow me to ask previously unanswerable questions.

Cell/Molecular biology

Neurons are a fascinating cell-class that use a variety of conserved molecular tools and signaling pathways to enable information processing. To describe and measure some of these components, I’ve used techniques such as cell culture, immunohistochemistry, PCR, transfection, cell fractionation, and protein identification.

Physiology

The immediate priority for the brain is to govern peripheral physiology. My doctoral work focused on plasticity in the neural systems that govern respiration, body temperature, blood pressure, and chemosensation.

GABA

As the brain’s primary inhibitory neurotransmitter, GABA shapes the timing and stability of neural networks. A subtype of tonic GABAA receptors, those containing ε or δ subunits, were the nucleus of my dissertation. From phylogenetic homology to pharmacology to transcriptional regulation, these molecules intoxicate me (did you catch that?).

Cortical Neurons

The most complex structure in the universe, the cortex is a self-organized, biological computer responsible for higher order cognition. I’ve worked with cortical neurons in culture, acute slices, and chronic recordings of single units in freely behaving animals (10+ days).

Chronobiology

From my graduate work on circannual and estrous cycles to my postdoctoral work on the role of sleep and wake in gating homeostatic and Hebbian plasticity in the cortex, I dwell on biological rhythms and the influence of the exterior world on the timing and expression of internal phenomena.
